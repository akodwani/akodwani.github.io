D.R.C.A

(05)

01

Where it started

DRCA started from me feeling bored with how often fashion seemed to recycle the same ideas. There were always new drops and new trend names, but a lot of it still felt like the same references being rearranged.

02

Why the name

DRCA stands for Disrupt, Revolutionize, Current Aesthetics. The name came from the ambition behind the project: not to claim that I had reinvented fashion, but to push against what felt visually predictable and test how an existing aesthetic could be taken somewhere less familiar.

03

What I wanted to test

I wanted to see whether I could build something that felt like its own world, not just another brand attached to an existing trend. The clothes, materials, lighting and casting needed to feel connected before anyone even saw the name.

04

Turning it into fashion

The main idea was to take the sharp, symbolic linework I was interested in and make it physical. It became chrome shoulder pieces, eyewear, raised seams and structures that wrapped around the body like armor or an exoskeleton.

05

The studio

I placed every character in the same dark space under one overhead light. The plinth made each person feel like a prototype or object being presented, rather than someone posing in a conventional fashion campaign.

06

Why I used AI

I started developing the campaign after seeing how far image and video generation had progressed. It gave me a way to test casting, styling, materials, silhouettes and camera ideas without first needing a large crew or physically producing the collection.

07

What DRCA became

The difficult part was not generating one interesting image. It was getting the same world to hold together across different people, outfits and angles. DRCA became a speculative campaign for a fashion world that does not physically exist yet, built through repeated direction, rejection and refinement.
